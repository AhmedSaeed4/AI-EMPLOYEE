# The hardest part of an AI employee isn't building it. It's getting humans to trust it.

Everyone optimizes the build. Almost nobody optimizes the adoption.

I've watched teams build brilliant digital FTEs that sit idle — not because they don't work, but because no one trusts the output enough to act on it. So the work quietly flows back to a human, and the AI becomes expensive shelfware.

Trust with an AI is earned the same way as with a new hire: in small, reversible steps. Skip the steps and you lose the team on day one.

**The trust ramp:**

**1. Shadow mode**
The AI runs alongside, the human still does the work, and you compare outputs afterward. Zero risk, zero excuses. This is where people learn what it's actually good at.

**2. Draft mode**
The AI produces the first version. A human edits, signs off, and owns it. Low risk — but it's where calibration happens. The team starts to feel where it's reliable and where it isn't.

**3. Owned mode**
The AI owns the task end-to-end and escalates only the exceptions. This is where hours actually compound.

Most teams skip straight to step 3. They get burned once, panic, and announce "AI doesn't work for us."

The capability was never the problem. The runway was.

What's the one task your team would trust an AI to own — if it proved itself first? 👇
